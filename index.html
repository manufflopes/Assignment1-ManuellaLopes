Tim Horton Biography | Ice Hockey Player

Miles Gilbert Horton, (January 12, 1930 – February 21, 1974), better known as “Tim” Horton thanks to a nickname his mother gave him before giving birth, was born on January 12, 1930, in Cochrane, Ontario, a mining region in the north of the province.

Horton stood out from the crows in many ways, including from the first time he laced up the skates. He enjoyed a successful junior career and was named the top defenseman in the Ontario Hockey Association in 1949 for his performance with the St. Michael’s Majors.

As a professional ice hockey defenceman, he played 24 seasons in the National Hockey League (NHL). He played for the Toronto Maple Leafs, New York Rangers, Pittsburgh Penguins, and Buffalo Sabres.

Horton married in 1952 with Lori Michalek of Pittsburgh, and had four daughters.

He died at age 44 following a single-vehicle crash in which drugs and alcohol were involved.

He’s known for more than just hockey, though you might know him as the most recognizable name in coffee and donuts in all of Canada, too.

As a 24-year veteran of the NHL and a successful businessman, he made an impact in more ways than one, and the legacy he left behind has made him remembered by millions.

In 2017, Horton was named one of the 100 Greatest NHL Players in history.

Career

Tim Horton was called "Superman" by goaltender Johnny Bower, who believed the defenseman and longtime teammate with the Toronto Maple Leafs could lift a filled 40-gallon oil drum. Another teammate, center Dave Keon, recalled that Horton could throw around railroad ties "like they were toothpicks." Fellow Toronto defenseman Bob Baun saw him barricade an intersection in Quebec as a prank by lifting barrels of cement. Gordie Howe of the Detroit Red Wings, hardly a weakling himself, called Horton "the strongest player in hockey."
Horton played his first game with the Maple Leafs in 1949. In 1952, he earned a regular spot on the team. He was a strong player and soon became known for his powerful slapshot (a shot made with a swinging motion).

Horton helped the Maple Leafs to win the Stanley Cup in 1962, 1963, 1964, and 1967. In 1970, he was traded to the New York Rangers. He joined the Pittsburgh Penguins the following year. From 1972 to 1974, he played for the Buffalo Sabres.
During his NHL career, Horton scored 115 goals and made 403 assists. He also was voted an All-Star six times. In 1977, he was inducted into the Hockey Hall of Fame.

For many people today, Tim Horton means doughnuts and coffee at the fast-food franchise he founded in 1964. But for over two decades, Horton's name meant skill, mobility and unsurpassed strength on defence in the NHL. Horton spent most of that time with the Maple Leafs, helping Toronto win four Stanley Cup championships. He was runner-up for the Norris Trophy twice and earned postseason All-Star recognition six times.

Horton played 1,446 games over 24 NHL seasons, scoring 115 goals, 403 assists, and 518 points. His 518 points rank 64th all-time among NHL defensemen, placing him comfortably among the greatest defensemen to ever play the game.
